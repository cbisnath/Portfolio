Remembering two giants of audio

This week the audio community is grieving the loss of two of its most formidable voices. On October 3, Richard Ferrone—known for his gravelly, compelling voice that was perfect for histories and mysteries alike, notably including the Lucas Davenport series—passed away after a battle with cancer. And on October 4, Dick Hill, the long-standing voice of the Jack Reacher series and the Nathan McBride series, among so many other high-stakes thrillers, also passed away after fighting cancer. While the world feels a little quieter now, we are so grateful for the many stories both men shared with us.

Nobel news

Félicitations are in order for Annie Ernaux, who just won the Nobel Prize in Literature. The committee cited the “courage and clinical acuity” that marks her work, which spans fiction, nonfiction, and fascinating blends of both. An influx of her English-translated titles came to audio in 2019—many performed by the Grammy-nominated and Audie-winning narrator Tavia Gilbert—making Ernaux one of the best French authors to enjoy in audio, whether you’re listening in French or English.

Recommended listening: Annie Ernaux

Last call for a swim in the Pacific

Well, to be accurate, you have another good 200-300 million years. That’s when scientists say the Pacific Ocean will close, forming the next supercontinent: Amasia (working title, we assume, since it’ll be our great-great-great-and-so-on descendants who will get naming rights). Apparently, every 600 million years or so, a new supercontinent is expected to form as plates shift and oceans close—and the Pacific, the oldest ocean on our planet, is the likely next culprit, as it has already been shrinking by a few centimeters per year. Since we won’t be around to see it, we’ll just have to look forward to the inevitable sci-fi novels about this new world with its new climate and culture, but in the meantime, we won’t take our West Coast sea for granted.

Recommended listening: Pacific

A long-awaited coming out story

Jinkies! After decades of hints and audience speculation, Scooby Doo’s Velma Dinkley is finally getting the coming out party she deserves. While previous writers and producers have as much as confirmed Velma’s sexuality in off-screen conversations and interviews, an upcoming addition to the franchise marks Velma's first on-screen depiction as a lesbian. Since we’re still lacking mainstream depictions of lesbians in most media, we’re thrilled that Velma is finally able to take her rightful place as Scooby Doo’s queer icon. It’s a win for queer creators and storytellers everywhere, and we can’t wait to see what the future holds for Velma’s character.

Recommended listening: The Vanishing Girl

Stranger than fiction (or parody)

In 2016, Anthony Novak was arrested for “using a computer to disrupt police functions” when he created a parody Facebook account of his local police station in Parma, Ohio. Though he was found not guilty, he is suing the city for allegedly violating his civil rights and filed a Supreme Court briefing for the case. His unlikely ally? The Onion. The satirical news organization filed a motion in his defense claiming his right to parody and satire, written in classic Onion-fashion with section headers such as “Parody Functions By Tricking People Into Thinking That It Is Real.” Looking for comedy that won’t get you into trouble? How about some of our Audible Original scripted comedies?

Recommended listening: The Onion Book of Known Knowledge
